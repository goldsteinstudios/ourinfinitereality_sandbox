## The Thesis

Paradoxes aren't contradictions. They're poles.

Being and non-being, one and many, same and different — these apparent opposites resolve when you see them as positions on a sphere rather than points on a line.

On a line, opposites exclude each other. On a sphere, opposites are connected by the surface between them. You can walk from one to the other without contradiction.

The "center" of the sphere — the paradoxical point where all opposites meet — can't be occupied. But it organizes everything around it.

## v5.5: Dimensionality from the Two-Branch Connection Problem

The v5.5 derivation chain provides the formal argument for **why** the sphere — and three dimensions — must arise.

The hyperbola xy = 1 has two disconnected branches. These branches cannot be connected within the 2D plane. A path between them requires a third dimension. The minimal isotropic closure is the sphere S², which can only be embedded in ℝ³.

This derives three spatial dimensions, not by assertion, but from the structure of the conservation constraint itself.

### The Three Equations

| Equation | Geometry | Role |
|----------|----------|------|
| xy = 1 | Rectangular hyperbola | Flat reciprocal constraint |
| x² + y² = 1 | Unit circle | Bridge between branches |
| x² + y² + z² = 1 | Unit sphere | Full isotropy |

Each equation IS the next level of the structural requirement: **constraint → rotation → isotropy**.
